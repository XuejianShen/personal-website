My CV

Xuejian(Jacob) Shen / 沈学简 / 沈學簡

I was a graduate student in the Theoretical Astrophysics group (TAPIR) at California Institute of Technology. My research advisor is Prof. Philip F. Hopkins. I work on theoretical cosmology and numeric simulations of galaxies.

During my Ph.D., I have the fortune to work on a diverse array of questions in theoretical astrophysics, related to the origins and the nature of dark matter, the formation and evolution of galaxies & supermassive black holes (SMBHs) as well as astrophysical fluid dynamics. These questions revolve on dramatically different physical scales in different epochs of the Universe, from the Big Bang 14 billion years ago down to the formation of our Milky Way galaxy and its satellites.

The nature of dark matter

My thesis research revolves around the nature of dark matter and relevant astrophysical probes. Given the strong disputes on the classical WIMP-like cold dark matter, a spectrum of alternative dark matter models has been proposed in the last twenty years. We use cosmological, hydrodynamical simulations to study galaxy formation & evolution in these alternative models, including but not limited to self-interacting dark matter (SIDM, elastic/dissipative), fuzzy dark matter (FDM, ultra-light axion-like-particles), classical QCD axions (and miniclusters formed in the post-inflationary scenario). The interplay of these beyond-standard-model physics with the known baryonic sector is also what we are interested in studying using simulations. For more detail, see my research pages.

Quasars and SMBH seeding

Quasars (quasi-stars) are interpreted as rapidly accreting, active supermassive black holes (SMBHs). The observations of quasars, or in general active galactic nuclei (AGN), have over fifty years of history. My first-year project at Caltech focused on the demographic properties of quasars in the early Universe (compiling multi-band observations and modeling bolometric quasar luminosity functions). In my senior years, I explored scenarios for seeding massive SMBHs in the early Universe with dissipative SIDM (the challenge to seed billion solar mass SMBHs at z≳7). For more detail, see my research pages.

Galaxy formation in the early Universe | Mock observations

In collaboration with the IllustrisTNG team, I developed a pipeline using the advanced dust radiative transfer code SKIRT to post-process simulated galaxies and generate galaxy UV-to-FIR SEDs and mock HST/JWST images. The pipeline has led to a series of papers on predictions for high-redshift galaxies, including UV/IR luminosity functions, emission line strength, spectral indices, dust attenuation curves and dust temperatures. Currently, we are studying early-Universe galaxy formation in alternative dark matter models and exploring observational signatures by applying the same pipeline on the THESAN simulations. In addition, with observers at Harvard CfA, we are training convolutional neural networks to identify galaxy mergers from JWST observations, based on mock galaxy merger images from TNG50. For more detail, see my research pages.
